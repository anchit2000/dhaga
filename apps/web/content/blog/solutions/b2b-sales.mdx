<Callout title="The short version">
A real B2B deal isn't a record in the CRM — it's a web of people who each care
about different things. Champions, economic buyers, blockers, influencers. That
map usually lives in your head or rots in stale CRM fields. Dhaga is a
**personal** relationship graph that captures every stakeholder, remembers the
personal and political context behind each one, and keeps them warm long after
the deal closes — including when your champion changes jobs and becomes your next
warm intro. It complements your team's system-of-record CRM; it doesn't replace
it.
</Callout>

## The deal is multi-threaded. Your memory isn't.

Ask any quota carrier why a deal slipped and you'll rarely hear "bad product." You
hear "we lost the champion," "procurement blindsided us," "the new VP didn't know
us." Enterprise deals are won and lost on the relationship map — who's the real
economic buyer, who quietly blocks, who influences the room, who owes whom a
favour.

Your CRM is built to report on the *deal*: stage, amount, close date. It is not
built to hold the *human* map. So the map lives in your head, and heads forget.
Six weeks into a stalled deal you can't remember whether the security lead was a
blocker or just cautious, or what the VP of Ops actually cared about in that first
call.

Dhaga's job is to hold that map for you. Capture each stakeholder as you meet
them and Dhaga turns your notes into a **private knowledge graph** — structured
people, companies, and the relationships between them. Every fact it derives keeps
a receipt back to the note it came from, so "the CFO is skeptical on ROI" is
always traceable to the call where you heard it, not a hunch you half-remember.

## Capture the room without breaking stride

You meet stakeholders in the worst possible conditions for good record-keeping —
mid-conference, walking out of a QBR, on a call you're actively running. So
capture has to be frictionless or it won't happen.

- **Scan a badge or business card** at the event and get a structured contact,
  not a photo you'll never revisit.
- **Voice-note the debrief** in the parking lot: "Met Priya, VP Eng at Acme,
  she's the technical champion, reports to the CTO who's the real budget owner,
  worried about migration risk." Dhaga extracts the people and the relationships.
- **Web quick-add or the browser extension** to grab someone from a profile while
  you're researching the account.
- **Import** an existing book from CSV or a LinkedIn export so years of contacts
  seed the graph on day one.

The point isn't data entry. It's that the moment you learn who reports to whom
and who cares about what, it lands somewhere queryable instead of evaporating.

## Remember what each stakeholder actually cares about

Multi-threading fails when you send the same pitch to everyone. The champion
wants the technical win; the economic buyer wants the business case; the blocker
wants to know it won't break their compliance posture. Dhaga lets you keep that
context per person — the professional stakes and the personal, political read —
as notes that become structured facts on each contact.

Then you ask, in plain English:

- "Who are the stakeholders in the Acme deal and where does each one stand?"
- "Who's the economic buyer at Acme and what do they care about?"
- "Which of my contacts at Acme haven't I spoken to in a month?"

**Natural-language recall answers only from your notes.** If you never captured
where the security lead stands, Dhaga tells you it doesn't know — it won't invent
a position. For a discipline where a confidently-wrong answer loses deals, "I
don't have that" is the honest and useful reply.

## Find the warm path in

Cold outreach into a new account is the hard way. The warm way is a path through
people you already know. Because Dhaga stores *who knows whom* — colleagues, past
coworkers, people who moved between companies — you can ask "who do I know at
Acme?" or "who in my network could introduce me to their VP of Sales?" and work
the introduction instead of the cold email.

That map is only as good as the relationships you actually captured, which is
exactly why capturing the room in the first place compounds over a career.

## Your champion just changed jobs — twice

This is the moment that separates a personal relationship graph from a company
CRM, and it's the single most valuable habit in enterprise sales.

Your champion — the person who fought for you internally — leaves Acme and lands
at a bigger company. In the CRM, they become a dead contact on a closed deal. In
reality they just became your **warmest possible lead** at a brand-new account.
They already trust you. They already know the product works.

Dhaga treats a person as a durable relationship, not a row attached to one deal.
Turn on the **opt-in watchlist** for the stakeholders who matter and Dhaga
surfaces signals like a role change, so "which champions changed companies this
quarter?" becomes a question you can actually answer — and act on while the
relationship is still warm. It's opt-in and bounded by design; you decide who's
worth watching, not the tool.

## Don't let closed-won or closed-lost go cold

The deal closing is the start of the relationship, not the end of it.

- **Closed-won** contacts are your renewal, your expansion, your reference calls,
  and your case studies. Forget them for a year and the renewal gets competitive.
- **Closed-lost** contacts are the deals that come back. "Not now" is not "never,"
  and the person who chose a competitor is often shopping again in eighteen months
  — especially after *they* change jobs.

Dhaga keeps both alive with pre-meeting briefs that pull together everything you
know before you re-engage, and follow-up draft assistance to make staying in
touch a two-minute task instead of a chore you skip.

## Where Dhaga fits next to your CRM

They do different jobs. Keep both.

| | Team CRM (system of record) | Dhaga (personal relationship graph) |
|---|---|---|
| Owns | Pipeline, forecast, deal stage, revenue | Your relationships and their context |
| Optimised for | Management reporting & process | Fast capture and personal recall |
| Stakeholder map | Contact fields on an account | A queryable graph of who-knows-whom |
| When you leave | Stays with the company | **Goes with you** — full export, any time |
| Champion who moved on | Dead contact on a closed deal | A warm intro at their new company |

The CRM answers "how's the pipeline?" for your manager. Dhaga answers "who do I
know, what do they care about, and how do I reach them?" for you.

## It's your network, wherever you work next

Reps change companies. When you do, the CRM stays behind — and so does every
relationship you logged in it. Dhaga is **privacy-first and yours**:
self-hostable, bring-your-own-API-key, with full export any time. The network you
spent a career building stays with you when you move on.

That's the whole idea — a thread you never have to drop.
