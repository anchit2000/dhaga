<Callout title="The short version">
In consulting, the relationship goes cold the moment an engagement ends — which
is precisely when it becomes most valuable. Your book of business isn't a
pipeline, it's a network: client stakeholders who move on to bigger roles, firm
alumni who turn into buyers, and the referral sources who send you the next
project. Dhaga is a private relationship memory for all of them: capture every
conversation in seconds, ask it plain-English questions like "who have I not
touched since the last project?", and let it quietly resurface the personal
context that makes a reconnect feel warm. It's your memory, not a firm-owned
CRM — your relationships stay yours across engagements and across firms.
</Callout>

Ask any partner where the next engagement comes from and the honest answer is
rarely "the pipeline." It's a former client who took a new role and remembered
you. A colleague who left the firm three years ago and now sits on the buying
side of the table. A referral from someone you did good work for once and stayed
in touch with. The book of business *is* the network — and the network decays the
day the invoice is paid.

Dhaga doesn't try to be a proposal engine or a delivery tool. It handles the
part that's genuinely hard and genuinely undervalued: remembering people, and
remembering *why they matter*, across hundreds of relationships and years of time.

## Capture the engagement, not just the org chart

During a project you meet everyone — the sponsor, the skeptic on the steering
committee, the sharp analyst who did all the real work, the CFO you presented to
twice. Six months later they're names you half-remember. The relationship lived
in the details, and the details evaporated.

Dhaga is built to capture those details with almost no friction, wherever the
work happens:

- **Scan a badge or a card** from a client site or a conference — a photo becomes
  a structured contact, so you're not typing at a reception desk.
- **Talk instead of type.** Walk out of a workshop and leave a voice note — "met
  the ops director, she's the real decision-maker, frustrated with the last
  vendor, wants a phased rollout, mentioned she's up for a bigger regional role" —
  and Dhaga turns spoken words into structured facts attached to that contact.
- **Quick-add from the web or the browser extension** when you find someone on
  LinkedIn or read that a client got promoted.
- **Import what you already have** from a CSV or a LinkedIn export, so your
  existing network comes with you on day one — not locked inside a firm system
  you'll lose access to when you move.

Capture takes seconds, in the moment, while the detail is still fresh — instead
of the debrief note you promise to write up and never do.

## Track stakeholders as they move across orgs

The most valuable thing about a client relationship is that it doesn't stay put.
The manager you impressed on a mid-size engagement becomes the VP who controls a
much larger budget somewhere else. But you only benefit if you notice — and
people rarely tell you.

Because every note becomes part of a private, queryable graph, Dhaga tracks the
structure of your network, not just a flat list of names: who moved where, who
reports to whom, who you met on which engagement. You can ask it in plain English:

- "Which former clients have moved into new buying roles?"
- "Who from the Meridian project is now at a different company?"
- "Who do I know at firms in the healthcare space?"

The answers come only from your own notes — and when Dhaga doesn't know
something, it tells you rather than making it up. For the moves you can't see
yourself, you can opt in to a bounded watchlist that flags role and company
changes for the people you care about, so a promotion becomes a reason to reach
out instead of an opportunity you learned about too late.

## Treat the alumni network as a pipeline

Every professional-services firm knows the truth quietly: your alumni are your
best future clients. People who left to go in-house remember who was good, and
they hire the people they trust. But most firms manage that asset with an annual
email and a LinkedIn group nobody reads.

Dhaga lets you treat individual alumni relationships as what they are — a warm
pipeline you can actually work:

| The relationship | What Dhaga remembers | Why it earns business |
|---|---|---|
| A colleague who left for a client-side role | Where they went, what they now own | They become a buyer who already trusts your work |
| An alum who changed companies again | The full trail of moves | You reconnect with context, not a cold "congrats!" |
| Someone you mentored years ago | That you had a real relationship | A genuine reason to reach out that isn't transactional |
| A former teammate's network | Who they know and worked with | Warm introductions instead of cold outreach |

You choose who to keep track of. The proactive, opt-in reminders surface the
people and moments you care about — they don't broadcast your network anywhere or
watch anyone behind your back.

## Business development that doesn't feel like selling

Partners who are good at BD rarely feel like they're selling. What they're
actually doing is staying genuinely present in people's professional lives — a
relevant note here, a well-timed check-in there — so that when a need arises,
they're the obvious call. The hard part isn't the charm; it's the *remembering*,
at scale, without a system.

That's the gap Dhaga fills:

- Ask **"who have I not touched since the last project?"** and get the list of
  relationships quietly going cold, before they go dark entirely.
- When it's time to reach back out, Dhaga can help you draft the follow-up,
  grounded in what you actually discussed, so it reads like a person who remembers
  them — not a template blast.
- Before a coffee or a reconnect call, pull together a short brief on that person
  from your own history, so you walk in sounding like you never lost touch.

None of this is automated outreach or a spray of sequences. It's the memory and
the timing that make a human reconnect land — you still decide what to say and
when.

## Remember the personal context that makes a reconnect warm

This is where relationship intelligence quietly wins the next engagement.
Reconnecting after two years is awkward when all you remember is a job title. It's
effortless when you remember the person.

Dhaga remembers the small things because you told it once:

- That their team was mid-reorg and they were stressed about headcount.
- That they were skeptical of consultants — and you won them over.
- The kids, the marathon they were training for, the city they were relocating to.
- The offhand comment that they'd "want help with the next phase eventually."

Every one of these facts keeps a **receipt** — a link back to the exact note it
came from — so you can trust where a detail came from and see the conversation
behind it. A reconnect built on real context doesn't feel like business
development. It feels like a colleague who genuinely remembered.

## Your network is your asset — and it stays yours

In a career that may span several firms, your relationships are the one thing you
truly carry with you. Dhaga is built on that principle. It's privacy-first and
self-hostable, you can bring your own API key, and you can export all of your data
at any time. It doesn't sell your contacts, mine them, or hand them to anyone
else. The proactive features are opt-in and bounded — a tool that works for you,
not a surveillance service pointed at your clients. When you change firms, your
network comes with you.
