<Callout title="The short version">
Advisory runs on trust compounded over decades, and trust is built from
remembered details — a client's grandkids by name, the retirement date they
mentioned in passing, the business sale that's two years out. Dhaga is a private
relationship memory that captures those details anywhere and lets you ask for
them in plain English before a review. It also holds the web around each
client: the CPA, the estate attorney, the center of influence who sent them your
way. One thing it is **not**: a compliance system or book of record. It sits
*alongside* your CRM and custodian systems of record — it never replaces them.

## The edge is memory, and memory doesn't scale in your head

Ask any advisor who's been at it twenty years what actually retains clients and
earns referrals, and it's rarely the portfolio. It's that you remembered the
daughter was applying to college, that you asked about the knee surgery, that
you knew the family cabin was the thing that couldn't be touched in a down
market.

That kind of memory is easy across ten households. It quietly breaks somewhere
past a hundred. The details don't disappear because you stopped caring — they
disappear because a spoken aside after a client dinner never made it into a
system, and six months later you can't recall which client it was.

Dhaga's job is to be the place those details land, and to give them back when
they matter. It is personal relationship memory, not a regulated record. Your
CRM and custodian remain the systems of record for accounts, holdings, and
anything that has to be reportable. Dhaga complements them by holding the human
context those systems were never built to carry.

## Capture the aside before it's gone

The details that matter almost never arrive at your desk. They come up in a
hallway, on a call, in the parking lot after a review. Dhaga is built to catch
them wherever you are:

- **Voice note** on the walk back to your car — "Meeting with the Ramirezes.
  Elena is retiring in March 2027, wants to sell the dental practice first.
  Grandson Mateo starting college next fall, they're funding it." Spoken becomes
  structured.
- **Business card or event badge scan** at a COI lunch or an industry
  conference — a photo becomes a contact, no manual typing.
- **Web quick-add or the browser extension** when you're reading a client's
  company news or a referral partner's bio.
- **CSV or LinkedIn-export import** to bring an existing book of relationships in
  without re-keying it.

Each capture becomes structured facts, not a wall of free text you'll never
re-read. And every AI-derived fact keeps a **receipt** — a link back to the
note it came from — so when Dhaga tells you Elena plans to retire in 2027, you
can see exactly which conversation that came from and when.

## Life events are the whole game

Wealth management is a business of triggers. A retirement, an inheritance, the
sale of a business, a liquidity event, a divorce, a new grandchild — these are
the moments where advice actually changes, and where a competitor's advisor is
just as likely to be circling. Missing one isn't just an awkward review; it's
the difference between deepening the relationship and losing the assets.

When you record these as you hear them, they become things you can *ask for*.
Dhaga's natural-language recall answers plain-English questions using only your
own notes:

- "Which clients have a liquidity event coming up in the next 18 months?"
- "Who mentioned selling a business this year?"
- "Which households have a child starting college next fall?"
- "Who's retiring in the next two years and hasn't had a plan review since?"

The important discipline: it answers **only from what you've captured**. If the
information isn't in your notes, it tells you so — it never fabricates a date or
invents a detail to fill the gap. In a field where being confidently wrong is
worse than being unsure, that restraint is a feature, not a limitation.

## The referral network is an asset — treat it like one

Your best growth channel usually isn't marketing. It's the CPA who trusts you
with their clients, the estate attorney you co-plan with, the center of
influence whose introduction opens a door cold outreach never would. That
network is a graph — who knows whom, who introduced whom — and most advisors
carry it entirely in their heads.

Dhaga turns it into a private knowledge graph you can query:

- "Who referred me the Patels?"
- "Which of my clients came through Sarah Chen, CPA?"
- "Which estate attorneys have I worked a case with in the last two years?"

Knowing that one COI has quietly sent you four households changes how you invest
in that relationship. Knowing a referral has gone cold tells you where to spend
a lunch.

## Walk into every review already prepared

Before a client meeting, Dhaga can pull an **opt-in pre-meeting brief** — what
you last discussed, the open threads, the personal details, the life events on
the horizon — assembled from your own notes so you're not re-reading a year of
history five minutes before they arrive. Afterward, **follow-up draft
assistance** helps you turn the conversation into a note and a next step while
it's fresh.

For the handful of relationships you actively track, an **opt-in watchlist** can
surface public signals about people you follow, and **user-triggered
enrichment** can pull public web context on a prospect or a referral partner
when you ask for it. Everything proactive is bounded and opt-in — nothing runs
against your book without you turning it on.

## What Dhaga is, and what it isn't

This distinction matters more in advisory than almost anywhere, so it's worth
being explicit:

| Dhaga is | Dhaga is not |
|---|---|
| Personal relationship memory for the human details | A compliance or supervision system |
| A private, queryable knowledge graph of people and referrals | A book of record or regulated recordkeeping tool |
| A capture layer for conversations and life events | Your custodian, portfolio, or account system of record |
| A complement that sits alongside your official CRM | A replacement for it |

Keep your reportable records where they belong. Let Dhaga hold the memory those
systems were never designed to keep.

## Your data, your control

Client relationships are among the most sensitive data an advisor holds — even
when it's the human context rather than the account numbers. Dhaga is
privacy-first: it's self-hostable, supports bring-your-own-API-key, and lets you
export all of your data at any time. Recall answers come from your notes and
nowhere else. Nothing about a client is enriched or sent to a cloud model unless
you choose it.
