Every founder eventually notices the same thing: the company is a machine for
turning relationships into outcomes, and *you* are the single point of failure.
The angel who'd write a check, the staff engineer who might leave BigCo for you,
the design partner whose feedback shapes the roadmap, the reporter who covers
your space — they're all one Rolodex, and that Rolodex is a tired human brain
being interrupted every fifteen minutes.

Dhaga isn't a pipeline tool or a sales CRM bolted onto your fundraise. It does
the part that's genuinely hard: remembering people, and remembering *why they
matter*, across five very different jobs your network is doing at the same time.

## Run the fundraise without losing the thread

A raise is a few dozen parallel conversations, each at a different stage, each
with its own promised follow-up. "Circle back when you hit $10k MRR." "Send me
the deck once the design partner signs." "I'm in if you find a lead." Miss one
of those and you don't get a rejection — you get silence, which is worse.

Capture each investor conversation the moment it ends, before the next one
starts:

- **Talk instead of type.** Walk out of the meeting and leave a voice note —
  "met Priya at Foundry, likes the wedge, wants to see the retention curve, said
  come back when we're at $10k MRR" — and Dhaga turns spoken words into
  structured facts attached to that contact.
- **Scan the card or badge** from a demo day or a conference so you're not
  retyping names at a stoplight.
- **Quick-add from the web or the browser extension** when a warm intro lands in
  your inbox.

Then ask, in plain English:

- "Which investors said follow up after we hit $10k MRR?"
- "Who's waiting on the updated deck?"
- "Who passed, and what reason did they give?"

The answers come only from your own notes — and when Dhaga doesn't know
something, it says so instead of inventing it. Before the next call, it can pull
a short brief on that investor from your own history, so you walk in remembering
exactly where you left off.

## Remember who intro'd whom

The intro graph *is* the fundraise. A warm intro from the right person is worth
ten cold emails, and forgetting who connected you to whom is how you fail to
close the loop — no thank-you, no update to the source, no second favour asked.

Because your notes become a private, queryable graph of people and the
relationships between them, "who intro'd me to whom" is something you can
actually recover. Ask "who introduced me to Priya?" or "which of my angels have
sent me the most intros?" and you get the connection, with the context attached.
When a raise closes, you know exactly who to thank — and who to send the first
update to next time.

## Recruit your first hires from your own network

Your best early hires are almost never on a job board. They're people you met
years ago and filed away with a mental note: "would be incredible if I ever
started something." The problem is that mental note doesn't survive eighteen
months of chaos.

Dhaga keeps the note for you. When you capture a conversation, capture the
signal too — "sharp infra person, unhappy at their current gig, said ping me if
you ever raise." Later, ask:

- "Who did I meet who might be open to an early engineering role?"
- "Which designers has anyone offered to introduce me to?"
- "Who mentioned they wanted to leave their current company?"

It turns a vague sense that "I know someone for this" into an actual list, drawn
from things you were genuinely told.

## Keep advisors and angels warm

Advisors and angels compound only if you use them, and using them means keeping
them in the loop — which is the first thing that falls off when you're heads-down
shipping. The relationship decays in silence, and by the time you need the
favour it feels cold to ask.

Dhaga treats staying in touch as something you can be deliberate about instead of
guilty about:

| The moment | What Dhaga remembers | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| An advisor made an offer | "Happy to intro you to two GPs" | You actually take them up on it before it expires |
| It's been a while | Who you haven't spoken to in months | A monthly update goes to the people who've gone quiet |
| An angel gave a steer | The advice, and whether you took it | You can close the loop and show you listened |
| A favour was returned | Who helped with what | You keep the ledger balanced, not one-sided |

The reminders are opt-in and bounded — Dhaga surfaces the people and promises
you asked it to watch. It doesn't broadcast your cap table or quietly surveil
anyone. You choose what to keep track of.

## Never forget which customer asked for what

Early customers are half revenue, half product team. One asked for SSO, another
for a CSV export, a third said they'd expand if you shipped an audit log. Lose
track of who asked for what and you either build the wrong thing or, worse, ship
the right thing and forget to tell the person who requested it.

Capture the ask against the customer, then let recall do the sorting: "who asked
for SSO?", "which design partners wanted the audit log?", "what did the last
three churned accounts complain about?" Every fact keeps a **receipt** — a link
back to the exact note it came from — so when you tell a customer "you asked for
this in March, it's live," you can trust the March.

## One brain, twenty context-switches, nothing dropped

The real founder condition isn't any single one of these jobs — it's all five at
once, interleaved, on a day you switched contexts twenty times. Investor call,
then a candidate, then a customer escalation, then a reporter, then back to the
deck. Human memory was not built for that, and pretending otherwise is how
threads get dropped.

Dhaga is the layer that survives the context-switching. Capture takes seconds and
happens in the moment — voice between meetings, a card at an event, a quick-add
from your desk — so the detail is still fresh instead of a note you'll write
"later" and never do. Recall is one plain-English question away. And the whole
thing is one graph, not five tools, so the investor who's also an advisor who
also intro'd you to a customer is a single person, remembered whole.
