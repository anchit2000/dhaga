## Rainmaking is relationship-keeping, not selling

The lawyers who build the biggest books rarely think of themselves as
salespeople. They think of themselves as people who stay in genuine contact —
who remember that a referring accountant just made partner, who check in on a
client between matters because they actually want to, who know which colleague
to introduce to a contact with an unmet need. The work is remembering, at scale,
across years.

That's exactly where it breaks down. A busy practice generates hundreds of
relationships — referral sources, current and former clients, opposing counsel
who become friends, people you met once at a bar-association dinner — and the
context that makes each one warm lives in your head, your inbox, and a stack of
cards. Heads forget, and a relationship you can't recall the details of is one
you quietly let go cold.

Dhaga's job is to hold that context for you. Capture the people and conversations
that matter and it turns your notes into a **private knowledge graph** —
structured contacts, the firms and relationships around them, and every fact you
recorded. Crucially, every AI-derived fact keeps a **receipt** back to the note
it came from, so "referred the Henderson matter" traces to the note where you
wrote it, not a vague sense that they sent you something once.

A note on scope: think of this as a personal professional-relationship layer, not
a case file. It's for the human context of your network — not privileged
communications, matter substance, or confidential client detail. Capture that
your referral source values quick turnarounds; don't capture the facts of the
matter.

## Nurture referral sources without keeping a spreadsheet

Referral sources are the engine of a book of business, and they're the easiest
to neglect — because there's no matter open to remind you they exist. The
accountant, the banker, the lawyer in an adjacent practice who sends you the work
they can't do: those relationships need tending precisely when nothing is
happening.

Capture them the moment you meet, in whatever conditions you're in:

- **Scan a business card or badge** at the CLE or the industry mixer and get a
  structured contact, not a photo you'll never open again.
- **Voice-note the debrief** on the drive home: "Met Dana, tax partner at
  Whitfield, sends estate-planning overflow, mentioned her firm's expanding into
  the coast." Dhaga extracts the people and the connections.
- **Web quick-add or the browser extension** to grab someone from a profile while
  you're researching a firm.
- **Import** years of existing contacts from a CSV or a LinkedIn export so the
  graph isn't starting from zero.

Then ask, in plain English: *"Which referral sources haven't I thanked or spoken
to recently?"* Dhaga answers **only from what you actually captured** — if you
never logged a conversation, it tells you it doesn't know rather than inventing
one. For reciprocity that has to feel sincere, an honest "you've gone quiet with
Dana" is worth more than a fabricated reminder.

## Stay in touch with clients between matters

The matter closes; the relationship shouldn't. The clients who come back — and
who refer others — are usually the ones who heard from you when there was no bill
attached. But "check in with everyone occasionally" is impossible to do from
memory across a full roster.

Because Dhaga treats a person as a durable relationship rather than a line item
tied to one engagement, a former client stays a warm contact long after the file
is closed. Keep the context that makes a check-in feel personal — that they were
about to hand the business to a daughter, that they follow a particular sports
team, that they mentioned a move — as notes that become facts on the contact.
Later you can ask *"which former clients haven't I spoken to this year?"* and
reconnect deliberately instead of by accident.

When you do reach out, **pre-meeting briefs** pull together everything you know
before a call, and **follow-up draft assistance** makes a warm note a two-minute
task instead of the chore you skip. The goal is to lower the friction of genuine
contact, not to automate the sincerity out of it.

## Cross-serve inside your own firm

Some of the best business you'll ever originate is work you personally can't do —
and the partner who can is three offices away. Cross-serving depends on knowing
both halves: what your contact needs, and who at the firm handles it.

Because Dhaga stores *who knows whom* and what each contact cares about, you can
ask *"who do I know at the client's new GC's office?"* or spot that a referral
source's expanding business is a fit for a colleague's practice. You work the
introduction from a real basis instead of a hopeful hunch. That map is only as
good as what you captured — which is exactly why frictionless capture compounds
over a career at a firm.

## Reconnect at the right moment

Timing is most of the art. The right note at a promotion, a firm move, or a
milestone lands; the same note six months late is noise.

Turn on the **opt-in watchlist** for the contacts who matter and Dhaga surfaces
signals like a role or firm change, so a referral source making partner or a
client's GC moving on becomes a reason to reach out while it's still relevant.
It's opt-in and bounded by design — you decide who's worth watching. You can also
run **enrichment** yourself, when you choose, rather than having the tool reach
out on its own.

## You control exactly what you capture

Because this is your network and your clients' trust, ownership matters. Dhaga is
**privacy-first and yours**: self-hostable, bring-your-own-API-key, with full
export any time. You decide precisely what goes in — keep it to the relationship
layer, leave privileged and matter-specific detail in the systems built for it —
and where it lives. If you ever change firms, the professional network you spent
a career building comes with you.

That's the whole idea — a thread you never have to drop.
