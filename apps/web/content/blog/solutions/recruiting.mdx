Great recruiting looks like luck from the outside. You "happen" to know the
right VP of Engineering the week a client needs one. In reality it's the payoff
of a conversation you had two years ago and remembered — the comp they wanted,
the two-hour commute they were done with, the vesting cliff they were waiting
out. The recruiters who win the long game aren't faster on the reach-out. They
remember more, for longer, about more people.

That memory is exactly what falls apart at scale. After a thousand screening
calls, the details blur. This is the gap Dhaga is built to close.

## The best candidates aren't in your ATS — they're in your head

Your applicant tracking system is a record of people who applied. The candidates
worth placing rarely apply. They're passive: heads-down, well-paid, not looking
— until the one Tuesday they are. Between now and that Tuesday, the entire
relationship lives in your memory and a scatter of notes.

Dhaga is built for capture wherever those touches happen. Right after a
screening call, record a **voice note** while it's fresh — "Priya, staff backend
at a fintech, happy technically but wants to move into management, would consider
remote-first only, comp expectation around senior-staff band, not active but
open in six months." Dhaga turns that into structured facts on Priya's profile.
Met someone at a meetup? **Scan their badge or card.** Working a sourcing list?
**Import a CSV or LinkedIn export.** Sitting at your desk? **Web quick-add or the
browser extension.** However the contact arrives, it lands in one place instead
of five.

The point isn't tidiness. It's that six months from now, when a role opens that
fits Priya, you can actually find her — and you remember *why* she's a fit.

## "Who wanted remote?" — ask in plain English

A knowledge graph you can't query is just a nicer filing cabinet. Dhaga's recall
works in plain English, answered **only from your own notes** — it doesn't invent
an answer it can't source.

Ask the things a recruiter actually needs:

- "Which backend engineers did I talk to who wanted remote?"
- "Who told me they'd be open to moving in the second half of this year?"
- "Which candidates are waiting out a vesting cliff, and when does it end?"
- "Who have I not contacted in nine months?"

That last one is the quiet killer of recruiting relationships. People don't go
cold because you stopped caring — they go cold because there's no system telling
you the thread is fraying. Dhaga's graph knows the last time you touched each
person, so a pipeline you'd otherwise forget stays a pipeline.

Every AI-derived fact keeps a **receipt** back to the note it came from. So when
Dhaga tells you Priya wanted remote, you can see the screening call it learned
that from — not a hallucinated summary, but your own words, dated. For work where
a wrong detail costs you a placement, that traceability matters.

## Today's "no" and yesterday's placement are your best future roles

Two of the most valuable groups in recruiting are the ones most systems throw
away:

**Silver-medalists.** The finalist who came second is often perfect for the
*next* search. But by then their file is buried under three closed roles. Keep a
note on why they were strong and where they fell short, and a plain-English
query surfaces them the moment a similar role opens — instead of you
re-sourcing from scratch.

**Past placements.** The engineer you placed two years ago is now a senior lead
with a team to build — and every reason to hire through the recruiter who got
*their* move right. The candidate becomes the client, and a warm one. But only
if you stayed in touch through the two years when there was no deal on the table.
Dhaga makes those years cheap to maintain: a light note after each catch-up, and
recall that reminds you who's due.

This is the two-sided nature of the business, and it's where a generic CRM
struggles. You're not tracking one pipeline. You're tracking **candidates and
hiring managers as one connected network** — and the same person moves between
the two sides over a career. Because Dhaga is a graph, the relationships between
them are first-class: who referred whom, who used to report to whom, which
manager you placed three people with. When a client asks for someone like the
engineer you placed last spring, you can trace the web instead of straining to
recall it.

## The moment a passive candidate becomes active

The single highest-value signal in recruiting is a **role change** — someone
getting promoted, joining a new company, or quietly updating a title. It often
means a person who was closed is suddenly open, or a former candidate now sits in
a seat that can hire you.

Dhaga's proactive intelligence is an **opt-in watchlist**: pick the people you
most want to stay ahead of, and Dhaga watches for role and company changes on
them. It's deliberately bounded — you choose who's on it, and it's a focused set,
not a firehose across your whole book. When something moves, that's your cue to
reach out with a reason, not a cold "just checking in."

Around each conversation, Dhaga can help you prepare and follow up. A
**pre-meeting brief** pulls together what you already know before a call so you
walk in remembering the vesting cliff and the two kids in school. User-triggered
**enrichment** and **follow-up draft assistance** help you move faster — always
on your command, never firing on its own or scraping in the background.

## Your network is your book of business — keep it yours

For a recruiter, the candidate and client network *is* the business. Handing it
to a platform that can change terms, price you up, or hold your data hostage is a
risk to the asset itself.

Dhaga is **privacy-first and self-hostable**. You can run it yourself, bring your
own AI API key so your notes never fund someone else's model business, and
**export everything at any time**. There's no enrichment happening quietly in the
background and no silent data collection — the AI acts when you ask it to. The
relationships you spent a decade building stay under your control, portable if
you ever move on.
